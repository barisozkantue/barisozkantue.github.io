How to Write a DSR Introduction

A step-by-step guide for junior researchers on structuring a Design Science Research paper introduction — from establishing relevance to justifying your methodology.

📄 6 moves ⏱ 500–800 words total 🎯 Design Science Research
1
Set the scene 2–4 sentences
Establish relevance
"Why should I care about this topic at all?"

Describe the real-world situation your paper is about. No definitions, no citations yet — just paint a picture of the domain and why it matters to organizations, practitioners, or society. Open a door; don't walk through it yet.

Example opening

"Organizations increasingly share data across institutional boundaries to unlock collaborative value, yet doing so in a trustworthy and sovereign manner remains an unresolved infrastructure challenge."

Avoid

Starting with "According to X, a dataspace is..." — definitions make readers passive immediately. Start with real-world consequence instead.
2
Name the problem 1 paragraph
Identify the need
"What is actually broken or missing?"

Zoom in to a specific, concrete problem. In DSR, this is usually that practitioners lack a tool, method, or framework that would help them do their job better. Be specific about who has the problem and under what conditions.

Example

"Organizations that want to participate in data sharing ecosystems have no structured guidance on how to design their participation in a way that is both technically workable and acceptable to all stakeholders involved."

Tip

A well-stated problem makes a practitioner nod and say "yes, that is exactly our issue." If it could apply to almost any topic, it is still too vague.
3
Review & gap 2–3 paragraphs
Spot the research gap
"Has nobody worked on this before?"

In two parts: first survey what prior work has addressed (Part A), then pivot with a contrast word — "however," "despite this," "yet" — to state what is still missing (Part B). This is the most citation-dense part of your introduction.

Three common DSR gap types

Prescriptive gap — prior work describes the problem but gives no design solution
Scope gap — existing artifacts address adjacent problems, not this specific combination of requirements
Validation gap — a solution exists but has never been tested or evaluated in this context
Avoid saying "little research exists on this topic" — reviewers will challenge it. Name the specific gap precisely.
4
State objective 1–2 sentences
Define your research objective
"So what will you actually do?"

Tell the reader exactly what you will build and evaluate. In DSR, always use action verbs — design, develop, construct, evaluate. Also name the artifact type: framework, model, method, instantiation, or design principles.

Example objective

"This paper develops and evaluates a framework that helps organizations design their participation in industrial data spaces, providing practical guidance for practitioners and new design knowledge for researchers."
The artifact you name here must directly solve the problem in Step 2. The reader should feel your objective is the obvious answer to the gap.
5
Justify DSR 1 paragraph
Position your methodology
"Why are you using this approach?"

Explain why DSR is the right methodology. The logic is simple: your goal requires creating something new, and DSR is specifically designed for research that produces useful artifacts while generating generalizable knowledge.

The three DSR pillars to always cite

Hevner et al. (2004) — the foundational DSR framework in Information Systems research
Peffers et al. (2007) — the six-step DSRM process model
Gregor & Hevner (2013) — knowledge contributions and positioning in DSR
Ask yourself: could I answer my objective with only interviews or a literature review? If no — because you need to build and test something — that is your justification.
6
Roadmap 1 paragraph
Sketch the DSR process & paper structure
"How does the rest of the paper unfold?"

Close with two things: how you applied DSR in this specific paper (which process or cycle you followed), and a section-by-section overview so the reader knows what is coming.

Example roadmap sentence

"Following Peffers et al.'s (2007) six-step DSRM process, this paper proceeds from problem identification through artifact design to evaluation via expert interviews. Section 2 reviews related work, Section 3 describes the methodology, Section 4 presents the artifact, Section 5 reports the evaluation, and Section 6 discusses contributions and limitations."

The logical thread

Domain matters → Something is broken → Prior work falls short → We will build X → DSR is why → Here is the map

Target length: 500–800 words for the whole introduction. Each step should flow logically from the previous one — if a reader can skip a step without losing the thread, tighten it.

Three things junior researchers often get wrong

✗

Starting with a definition. "According to X, a dataspace is..." makes readers passive immediately. Start with real-world consequence and pull them in first.

✗

A gap that is too broad. "Little research exists on this topic" will be challenged by reviewers. Be precise: this specific combination of requirements, this artifact type, this context.

✗

Steps that do not connect. The problem should follow naturally from the relevance. The gap from the problem. The objective from the gap. A broken logical chain means a weak introduction.

Key DSR references

2004 Hevner, March, Park & Ram — "Design Science in Information Systems Research." MIS Quarterly, 28(1). The foundational framework for DSR.
2007 Peffers, Tuunanen, Rothenberger & Chatterjee — "A Design Science Research Methodology for Information Systems Research." Journal of MIS, 24(3). The six-step DSRM process.
2013 Gregor & Hevner — "Positioning and Presenting Design Science Research for Maximum Impact." MIS Quarterly, 37(2). How to frame DSR contributions.
2014 Wieringa — Design Science Methodology for Information Systems and Software Engineering. Springer. Useful if your work leans toward engineering design.
